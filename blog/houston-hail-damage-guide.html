Houston Hail Damage Roof Guide:
What To Look For & What To Do

From micro-fractures in the Katy corridor to soft-impact dimples in Pearland—learn how to spot, document, and claim actual storm hail impact before leaks occur.

📅 May 17, 2026 ⏱️ 7 Min Read 💎 Professional Roofing Intel

Introduction

When cold atmospheric air masses collide with dry West Texas currents over the warm waters of our Gulf coast, the result is often intense supercell storms. For Southeast Texas residents, these weather pattern changes mean localized, high-velocity hail events. Because hail strikes rapidly and inconsistently, homeowners are frequently left with severe, undetected damage to their homes' primary defense line.

This comprehensive field guide explains how varying hail stone diameters affect differing rooftop substrates (asphalt shingle, standing seam metal, natural tile). We highlight regional microclimates like the Katy-Cypress corridor, detail how to recognize hidden structural damage, and outline the exact steps required to execute successful storm restoration projects of commercial-grade quality.

How Hail Attacks Different Roofing Materials

Hail damage does not look uniform across all properties. It varies extensively based on the size of the hailstones and the material of your roofing assembly:

1. Asphalt Shingles (Bruising and Granule Delamination)

When a solid chunk of ice striking at terminal velocity impacts an asphalt-fiberglass shingle, it compresses the underlying fiberglass mat. This impact immediately strips away the coarse slate protective granules, embedding the material directly into the pliable bitumen layers. This leaves dark, soft indentations resembling bruised fruit, hence the industry term: "shingle bruising." Over the following weeks, this broken asphalt layer is exposed to raw UV rays, leading to rapid cracking and structural degradation.

2. Metal Roofing (Micro-Dent Arrays and Interlocking Stress Points)

Standard standing seam and commercial corrugation systems resist structural water ingress exceptionally well, but are still vulnerable to localized cosmetic and structural damage. Large hailstones (exceeding 1.5 inches in diameter) leave deep, visible dimples in metal panels. If the hail impact occurs near interlocking standing seam joins or mechanical fasteners, it can compromise the critical weather seals and lead to slow structural leaks.

3. Tile and Slate (Craters, Fractures, and Spalls)

Clay tile and natural slate are highly durable but brittle. Sudden impact from large hail will shatter tiles. This impact creates star-shaped cracks, corner spalls, or large structural holes. Broken tile fragments often slide out of position, exposing the underlying waterproof membranes directly to heavy rainfall.

⚠️ The Danger of Wind-Driven Hail

When a storm combines 60+ MPH wind gusts with large hailstones, the angle of impact shifts from vertical to nearly horizontal. This directs concentrated kinetic energy against shingle vertical joints, gable flashing, and structural ridge caps, multiplying the overall damage.

Visible vs. Unseen (Hidden) Damage

Many homeowners assume their roof is perfectly fine simply because they do not see shingles missing or water pooling on their garage ceilings. This is a dangerous misconception.

Visible Damage: This is immediately apparent from ground-level assessments. It includes dented aluminum gutters, shattered plastic attic vents, cracked vinyl siding, and shattered skylight domes.

Hidden Damage: This requires direct, physical hands-on examination by a certified HAAG forensic inspector. Hail impact can crack the underlying fiberglass backing of an asphalt shingle while leaving the surface granules relatively intact. This creates a hidden micro-fracture that expands during daily heat cycles, eventually allowing water to seep through and begin rotting the wooden deck boards.

The Katy-Pearland Hail Corridor: Local Storm Data

In Southeast Texas, the "Katy-Cypress-Pearland Corridor" is recognized by local weather organizations as a high-risk area for severe hail storms. As cold fronts sweep down toward the warm waters of the Gulf, they frequently form intense, linear supercell storms that cross the Katy region and track southeast over Sugar Land and Pearland.

How To Navigate Your Texas Insurance Hail Claim Successfully

If your property is impacted by hail, navigating the insurance process can feel overwhelming. Follow this proven, professional 5-step workflow to ensure your claim is handled correctly:

  • Step 1: Document the Event. Write down the exact date and time the storm hit. Capture photographs of hail stones resting on your lawn, deck, or driveway next to a standard reference object like a coin or measuring tape.
  • Step 2: Get a Forensic Inspection First. Before calling your insurance carrier, have a certified roofing contractor conduct a thorough roof inspection. They will provide a detailed physical report documenting any actual damage, which helps justify your formal insurance claim.
  • Step 3: File Your Official Claim. Contact your insurance company to report the hail damage. Provide the storm date and the physical report from your contractor to initiate the process.
  • Step 4: Coordinate the Adjuster Meeting. Your insurance company will schedule an adjuster to physically examine your roof. Make sure your certified roofing contractor is present during this meeting to represent your interests and point out hidden damage.
  • Step 5: Review the Estimate and Start Work. Once the claim is approved, review the itemized scope of work with your contractor. Remember that in Texas, roofing contractors are legally required to collect your insurance deductible (HB 2102).
