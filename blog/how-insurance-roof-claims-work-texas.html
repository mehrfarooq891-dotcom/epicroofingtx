How Roof Insurance Claims Work in Texas:
Complete Houston Guide

Demystifying Texas insurance laws, House Bill 2102 deductible regulations, and the exact step-by-step process of securing approval for high-wind and hail damage repairs.

📅 May 17, 2026 ⏱️ 9 Min Read ⚖️ HAAG Legal & Technical Advisory

How long does a roof insurance claim take in Texas?

In Texas, a standard roof insurance claim typically takes 30 to 60 days from initial report to actual physical installation. Texas law forces insurance carriers to acknowledge and assess claims within 15 days of filing, and either accept or deny claims within 15 business days of receiving all proof-of-loss documents.

What does a Texas insurance adjuster look for?

A Texas adjuster evaluates structural roofs for verifiable natural hazards (e.g., matching impact patterns of hail bruises or wind creasing) instead of normal wear-and-tear or mechanical damage. They look for specific circles of impact within a 10x10 square foot "test square" across each directional face of the roof structure.

Texas Insurance Law Basics (House Bill 2102)

Before beginning any roof restoration project in the state of Texas, homeowners should understand local legal requirements. On September 1, 2019, Texas House Bill 2102 went into effect, changing property insurance regulations.

Under HB 2102, it is a Class B misdemeanor for a roofing contractor to pay, waive, rebate, or absorb a homeowner's required insurance deductible. This means any contract offering to "waive your deductible" or pay it through "advertising credits" is illegal. Homeowners are legally required to pay their full deductible, and insurance companies now require proof of payment—such as a bank transaction, canceled check, or approved financing plan—before releasing final depreciation payments.

Working with a contractor who follows state law protects you from legal risks and ensures your replacement meets local building codes and windstorm certification requirements.

The 8-Step Roof Claim Process

Filing a property claim does not have to be stressful. Follow this clear 8-step process to ensure a successful outcome:

  1. Comprehensive Physical Assessment Have a qualified roofing professional inspect your entire roof. They will verify whether the damage is severe enough to justify a claim, helping you avoid unnecessary claims on your record.
  2. Initial Loss Report If verified damage exists, contact your insurance company's claims center. Provide the exact date of the storm event and have your policy number ready.
  3. Official Claim Number Assignment Your carrier will generate an official claim tracking number. Write this number down, as it will be used for all future documents and payments.
  4. Adjuster Meeting Coordination An insurance adjuster will be assigned to inspect your roof. It is highly recommended to have your roofing contractor present during this meeting to ensure all damaged areas are identified.
  5. Detailed Scope of Work (Xactimate) Evaluation Your insurance carrier will issue an itemized scope of work detailing approved repairs. Your contractor will review this document to ensure the pricing matches local labor and material costs.
  6. Initial ACV (Actual Cash Value) Payment Your carrier will issue a first check based on your roof's age-adjusted value, minus your deductible. This initial payment allows your contractor to order materials and schedule the project.
  7. Complete Roof Replacement Installation Our skilled crews will remove your old roof and install a modern, high-grade roofing system. We document every step of the installation with clear photos for your records.
  8. Final Depreciation Release Once the project is complete, your contractor will send a final invoice to your insurance carrier. The carrier will then release the remaining withheld funds to complete payment.

What Adjusters Look For During Inspections

Insurance adjusters search for clear, physical proof of storm impact. For hail claims, they document the number of distinct bruises within a 100-square-foot "test square" on each side of your roof. They also check for secondary signs of storm impact, such as dented air conditioning units, damaged window screens, and chipped paint on fences.

For wind claims, adjusters look for shingles with broken sealant lines or visible creases along the top edge, indicating they were lifted by high winds. Normal wear-and-tear, blistering from poor ventilation, or mechanical damage from foot traffic is typically not covered under standard policies.

Common Homeowner Mistakes to Avoid

Avoiding these common pitfalls can make a big difference in the success of your project:

  • Waiting Too Long to File: Most insurance policies require claims to be filed within one year of the storm event. Waiting too long can lead to denied claims or expensive leaks.
  • Choosing a Low-Bid Contractor: Working with contractors who offer extremely low bids or promise to waive your deductible is risky and often leads to low-quality work or legal issues.
  • Signing Over Your Claim Rights: Be cautious about signing documents like an Assignment of Benefits (AOB) before your claim is approved. Read the fine print carefully before agreeing to any terms.
